01 — Context

The environment I was operating in

Nigeria's WASH sector operates across 36 states and 774 local government areas (LGAs), with funding flowing from international donors — primarily UNICEF, FCDO, the EU — through federal ministries and down to state-level implementing agencies. Budget execution data was the heartbeat of every Joint Sector Review (JSR), the annual process through which government and donors assess sector performance and make allocation decisions affecting millions of Nigerians' access to clean water and sanitation.

As Product Manager for IT and Data, I owned the digital and data products underpinning that entire decision-making system. The budget intelligence platform described here was the most impactful of 10 national and sub-national digital data products I delivered across 8 years at UNICEF Nigeria.

"Before this platform existed, the JSR preparation cycle required 6–8 weeks of manual data compilation. State officers were spending more time building spreadsheets than interpreting the data inside them."

02 — The Challenge

What was broken, and why it mattered

The core problem was systemic invisibility. Budget execution data existed — but it lived in incompatible formats, updated on different schedules, and was owned by different actors with different incentives to share it. By the time data was compiled, cross-checked, and formatted for review, it was already 6–8 weeks old.

Budget execution gaps went undetected until the end of the fiscal cycle. Underspent funds that could have been reallocated sat idle. Overspent lines triggered last-minute funding shortfalls. The system had no early warning mechanism.

I identified 4 compounding failure modes through initial stakeholder conversations:

Pain Point 01

Manual data compilation: State programme officers averaged 4+ hours per week copy-pasting data across Excel files with no audit trail, creating constant version conflicts.

Pain Point 02

Siloed data sources: Ministry financial data, WASH monitoring data, and donor reporting systems were never reconciled in real time — or at all before the JSR.

Pain Point 03

No execution alerts: There was no mechanism to flag when budget execution dropped below critical thresholds (e.g., below 60% of planned spend by Q3).

Pain Point 04

Inaccessible for non-analysts: Existing reports required Excel skills to navigate. Ministry directors and government counterparts could not self-serve any data insights.

Jobs-to-be-Done synthesis

Three core JTBD statements emerged from my synthesis of discovery findings:

Programme Officer

"When I'm preparing for the JSR, I need to see budget execution vs. plan across all states in one view, so I can identify gaps before they become crisis items on the review agenda."

Ministry Planner

"When I'm reporting to the Director, I need to show LGA-level progress without needing to open 12 Excel files, so I can brief accurately and without preparation time."

Donor Analyst

"When I'm validating spend against our commitment, I need data that is traceable and consistent, so I don't have to reconcile UNICEF data with ministry data manually."

04 — Solution

What we designed and built

The solution was a Power BI-based budget intelligence platform connecting 3 live data sources through a centralized data pipeline. I owned the product definition: data model design (ERD), indicator dictionary, feature specifications, and user acceptance criteria. I worked with 2 data engineers and a GIS analyst on the technical build, and engaged government IT counterparts at the state level for deployment and access provisioning.

    Multi-state budget vs. actuals view — Real-time execution tracking across all states and LGAs with configurable date ranges and spending categories, replacing the 8-week manual compilation process.
  • ✓
    LGA-level drill-down — Hierarchical navigation from national → state → LGA → intervention level, accessible to non-technical users via click-through, no Excel required.
  • ✓

  • ✓

05 — Delivery

How we shipped it

Delivery ran as a hybrid agile-waterfall engagement: 2-week sprints for the build phases, waterfall-style gates for government IT approvals and data-sharing agreements. The biggest constraint was not technical — it was institutional. Getting 3 separate organizations to agree on a shared data schema took 6 of the 16 total weeks.

Weeks 1–4
Discovery & Data Audit
Stakeholder interviews (18 sessions), current-state data flow mapping, data quality audit across 3 source systems. Identified 4 critical data gaps requiring upstream standardization before pipeline build.
Weeks 5–8
ERD Design & Schema Agreement
Designed data model and indicator dictionary. Facilitated 3 cross-organizational working sessions to align on shared field names, update frequencies, and data ownership. Produced engineering-ready ERD and pipeline spec.
Weeks 9–14
Build — ETL Pipeline + Dashboard
Worked with 2 data engineers on SQL pipeline build and Power BI dashboard development. Ran weekly sprint reviews with a core group of 5 end users. Conducted 2 rounds of usability testing at state level — revised navigation architecture after round 1 feedback.
Weeks 15–16
UAT, Training & Handover
Ran structured UAT sessions with 12 end users across 3 states. Delivered training to 28 government and UNICEF staff. Produced user guide and data governance documentation. Platform went live before Q3 budget review cycle.
Challenge How I resolved it
Government IT restrictions blocked direct DHIS2 API access Negotiated a scheduled data export agreement with ministry IT, feeding into a secure SharePoint intermediary layer
Inconsistent field naming across 3 data sources Produced a cross-source indicator dictionary and ran a 2-day data harmonization workshop with data custodians
Low digital literacy among some state-level users Added a simplified 1-page "dashboard summary" view with plain-language annotations, alongside the full analyst view
Connectivity constraints in remote LGA offices Built offline-cached PDF export option; Power BI mobile app deployed on government tablets

07 — Reflection

What I would do differently

Every product teaches you something. These are the 3 things I would approach differently if I were building this today:

Data instrumentation

I would instrument usage analytics from week one — not wait until post-launch adoption raised questions. Knowing which dashboard views were actually being used (versus which we assumed were valuable) would have sharpened iteration decisions significantly.

Earlier field inclusion

LGA-level users (the people doing the actual data entry) were brought in too late in the process. Their feedback in UAT prompted a navigation redesign that should have happened in sprint 2, not sprint 8. Discovery needs to start at the furthest point of the user chain.

Ownership plan

I should have started the handover planning at project kickoff, not week 14. The product was sustained because I advocated for it internally — but a formal product ownership and data stewardship plan would have reduced that dependency on me personally.
